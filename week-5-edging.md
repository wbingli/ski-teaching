# Week 5: Edging Control

**PSIA Fundamental 3**: Control edge angles through a combination of inclination and angulation.

**Goal**: Develop ability to control ski edge angles for grip, turn shape, and speed control.

---

## Why This Matters

When edge angles are controlled properly:
- **Grip on snow** - edges cut into snow instead of sliding
- **Turn shape control** - higher edge = tighter turn possible
- **Speed control** - edging + pressure = braking effect
- **Carving** - clean arcs instead of skidding

### Edge Angle Effects

| Edge Angle | Result |
|------------|--------|
| Flat | Ski slides sideways (sideslip) |
| Low angle | Skidded/brushed turns |
| Medium angle | Shaped turns with some skid |
| High angle | Carved turns (tail follows tip) |

---

## The Body Movement

> Lower legs incline toward the inside of the turn while the upper body tilts (angulates) toward the outside. This combination creates edge angle while maintaining balance.

### Two Ways to Create Edge Angle

**1. Inclination**
- Whole body tips toward inside of turn
- Simple but limited - too much = falling over
- Like a motorcycle leaning in a turn

**2. Angulation**
- Creating angles at joints (ankle, knee, hip)
- Upper body stays more upright
- Allows higher edge angles while balanced
- "Breaking" at the waist toward outside of turn

**Key Cue**: "Knees into the hill, shoulders level"

### Common Errors & Fixes

| Error | Cause | Fix |
|-------|-------|-----|
| Skidding | Edge angle too low | Commit more to edge |
| Catching edges | Edge angle too high for speed | Reduce angle or add speed |
| Banking (no angulation) | Only using inclination | Hip angulation focus |
| A-frame | Over-edging inside ski | Focus on outside ski edge |
| Upper body leaning in | Counter-productive angulation | "Shoulders level" cue |

## PSIA Concepts Explained

### Inclination vs. Angulation

| | Inclination | Angulation |
|-|-------------|------------|
| **What** | Whole body tips | Joints create angles |
| **Where** | Entire body | Ankle, knee, hip |
| **Limit** | Falls over at extreme | Can maintain balance |
| **When used** | Low edge angles, fast | High edge angles, slow/medium |

### The Blending
Most skiing uses **both** inclination AND angulation:
- Inclination gets you started
- Angulation increases edge angle while staying balanced
- Amount varies by speed, turn shape, terrain

### Edge-Pressure Relationship

- **More pressure** on an edged ski = more grip
- **Less pressure** on an edged ski = easier to pivot/slide
- This is why we learned pressure (Week 3) before edging (Week 5)
- Outside ski: high pressure + high edge = carving

### Reading the Tracks

After your run, look at your tracks in the snow:
- **Clean lines** = good edging
- **Smeared/wide** = skidding
- **Two parallel lines** = both skis carving
- **One line only** = inside ski not doing much

## Side Slip to Edge Set (Versatility Task)

This is a **PSIA Level 1 required task**:

1. Stand across hill, skis pointing perpendicular to fall line
2. Slight flex, tipped on uphill edges
3. Extend to flatten skis â†’ slip sideways down hill
4. Flex slightly, move knees uphill â†’ edge to control speed
5. Flex more â†’ stop completely

**Key Points**:
- Slip directly down (no forward/back drift)
- Skis stay perpendicular to fall line
- Smooth transitions between slip and set

## Dry Land Workout

This week builds lateral stability - essential for strong edge control!

**1. Side Plank** (20 seconds each side x 2 sets)
- Lie on side, prop up on forearm, lift hips off ground
- Keep body in straight line from head to feet
- *Why it helps*: Lateral core strength supports angulation movements

**2. Lateral Hops** (10 hops each direction)
- Stand on one foot, hop sideways to land on the other foot
- Stick each landing with control before hopping back
- *Why it helps*: Builds lateral power and stability for edge-to-edge transitions

**3. Calf Raises** (15 reps x 3 sets)
- Stand on edge of step, rise up on toes, lower heels below step level
- Control the movement both up and down
- *Why it helps*: Strong calves support ankle movements for edge control
