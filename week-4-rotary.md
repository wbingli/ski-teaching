# Week 4: Rotary Control

**PSIA Fundamental 4**: Control the skis' rotation (turning, pivoting, steering) with leg rotation, separate from the upper body.

**Goal**: Develop independent leg steering while maintaining a stable, quiet upper body.

---

## Why This Matters

When rotary is controlled properly:
- **Turn shape flexibility** - can turn tighter or wider than ski's natural radius
- **Upper body stability** - better balance and vision
- **Efficient movement** - legs do the work, not whole body
- **Adaptability** - essential for bumps, steeps, variable terrain

### The Problem with Upper Body Rotation
- Throws weight to inside of turn (bad for pressure)
- Disrupts balance
- Creates inefficient "windshield wiper" skiing
- Makes it harder to link turns

---

## The Body Movement

> Legs rotate in the direction of the turn while the upper body stays generally aligned with the intended path down the hill. This creates separation between lower and upper body.

**Key Cue**: "Turn your feet, not your shoulders" or "Belly button faces down the hill"

### What to Watch For

| Good Signs | Needs Work |
|------------|------------|
| Upper body faces general fall line | Shoulders rotating with skis |
| Legs visibly rotating | Whole body rotating as unit |
| Pole tips stay forward | Poles swinging side to side |
| Ski lead change occurs | No lead change (skis parallel throughout) |

### Common Errors & Fixes

| Error | Cause | Fix |
|-------|-------|-----|
| Counter-rotation | Over-rotating upper body opposite | Reduce movement, find neutral |
| No lead change | Insufficient leg rotation | Pivot slips, exaggerate |
| Banking (whole body leaning) | Not using leg rotation, just leaning | Pole drag drill |
| Square hips | Not enough rotation | Active femur rotation focus |

### Ski Lead Change

The **ski lead change** is key indicator of rotary:
- At turn initiation, the new outside (uphill) ski is slightly ahead
- Through the turn, skis become even, then new inside pulls ahead
- This happens naturally with proper leg rotation
- **No lead change** = insufficient rotary movement

## PSIA Concepts Explained

### Rotary vs. Rotation

**Rotary** = The skill of controlling ski direction through leg movement
**Rotation** = The actual turning movement

We want:
- **Active rotary** in the legs
- **Minimal rotation** in the upper body

### Types of Rotary Movement

1. **Steering** - Gradual, continuous rotation of legs through turn
2. **Pivoting** - Quick rotation, usually at slow speeds
3. **Guiding** - Light steering to shape the turn

### Where Rotary is Most Important

| Turn Phase | Rotary Role |
|------------|-------------|
| Initiation | Pivot/steer tips into fall line |
| Shaping | Guide turn arc with continuous steering |
| Finish | Steer across fall line for speed control |

### Separation (Counter)

- Upper body "counters" the leg rotation
- Creates torsion through the core
- Allows quick transitions between turns
- More separation needed in:
  - Short radius turns
  - Steep terrain
  - Bumps

## Dry Land Workout

This week targets core stability - the foundation for upper/lower body separation!

**1. Plank Hold** (30 seconds x 3 sets)
- Forearms and toes on ground, body in straight line
- Keep core tight, don't let hips sag or pike up
- *Why it helps*: Core stability allows upper body to stay quiet while legs work

**2. Standing Hip Circles** (10 circles each direction)
- Stand on one leg, make circles with the other leg's knee
- Keep upper body still while hips rotate
- *Why it helps*: Develops hip mobility and separation from upper body

**3. Bird-Dogs** (8 each side x 2 sets)
- On hands and knees, extend opposite arm and leg
- Keep core stable, don't let hips rotate
- *Why it helps*: Trains core to resist rotation - key for quiet upper body skiing
